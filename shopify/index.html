DIVE Networks

DIVE Networks focused on outputting an internal TV stream of custom content for companies, highlighting relevant and insightful information informed by trends and social posts. As the UX Lead, I oversaw widgets design from conception to delivery, and helped develop the front-end code as well.

DIVE Network sample images on various devices.

DIVE's product space is quite unique as it was a mainly visual and non-interactive consumable product. As a result, I had to conduct research and refer to industry standards when designing each of the interfaces in the video playlist. Sizing, color contrast, timing, and speed, were all taken into consideration when creating new widgets. Custom animation and layouts were also used to increase visual interest and to help users quickly differentiate between topics.

DIVE Live Screenshot.

DIVE also had a sibling product called Team DIVE, which was a responsive web app that not only included the player mentioned above, but also had the added functionality of allowing users to individually select and explore data from the library of social media and brand information available to them. I designed and helped build this entire project, involving: defining the project requirements and needs analysis; designing the information architecture; creating wireframes & prototypes; and building & polishing the front-end app.

Crowd Curio

Crowd Curio is a crowd sourced platform that allows everyday people or "citizen scientists" to contribute to scientific research. Similar to sites like Galaxy Zoo, it relies on users to help sort and catalog data that would be otherwise very difficult or impossible to do (at this point in time) with machines. Crowd Curio as a platform was meant for anyone and everyone to be able to share, conduct, and contribute to experiments across the world. As the designer and front-end developer, I helped design the layout and configuration of web app.

Crowd Curio Wireframes.

Crowd Curio initially had several requirements:

  • allow for the uploading & management of experiments
  • create custom modules/interfaces for the different types of topics
  • gamify the experiments to increase engagement
  • make new experiments easily discoverable

    • In order to satisfy these needs, I started off with several paper wireframes and a site map, which I then converted to an HTML prototype after design improvements.

    iOS App - Ash City Catalog

    Ash City (now AlphaBroder) is a clothing manufacturer and distributor in Canada and USA for large scale orders. Since fashion changes quickly, inventory styles and stock must follow, so the need for an easily accessible way to preview and order arose. I created an iOS app so that the sales team and potential customers would be able preview and order from the Ash City catalog.

    The requirements for this project were straight forward: pull in the up-to-date catalog styles, and provide item details such as product description, color/sizing options, and detailed images. Existing customers also needed to be able to log in so that they could to check pricing, inventory, and order.

    I started off with a clear list of project requirements to ensure that all targets were visible and achievable within our 4 month time-frame. I then created paper prototypes and wireframes, following up with an information architecture document to connect all the pieces together. Iterations and revisions were completed along the entire process to ensure that both the sales team and potential customers were able to understand and use the app. In addition, to designing and coding the app, I also created all of the image assets and the app icon.

    This was a hybrid role as I was the sole manager, designer, and developer for this project. By the end of 4 months, I successfully completed the app and got it approved on the Apple App Store. Within 2 years, the app reached 10k+ downloads and received 20k+ orders. For this project, I used Adobe Photoshop & Illustrator, and Objective C.

    Yelp Website Redesign

    Yelp is a popular site for exploring and searching for new venues. I was using Yelp's website one day back in August of 2014 and I was frustrated with it's overwhelming functions and buttons, when all I wanted to search for was nearby restaurants. I decided to redesign the website to eliminate the clutter and give more direct and faster access to users.

    As with many businesses, Yelp follows the 1/9/90 rule – i.e. 1% of users actively create content; 9% participate via commenting, rating, etc; 90% lurk. By extrapolating Yelp's Dec 31, 2014 fact sheet of reviewed businesses by category, we can assume over 50% of total visitors used Yelp for looking up stores, restaurants, and services (this would further change by locale, as Yelp's usage would differ based on its local presence). Focusing the majority of users is what I've kept in mind with the redesign.

    Yelp's Reviewed Businesses by Category, Dec 2014.

    For the header, the initial location is detected and set via IP address. As it is uncommon to change locations often, and has been moved to the top left rather than placed beside the search bar. The sorting categories are now more conspicuous, and the locations less so. The header has decreased in height, and the second row has been restyled to fit in with the tabs on the right side.

    The Updated Header.

    The previous 13 search categories have been replaced with only 4 to reduce visual clutter. These tabs remain static and change the content of the entire page. The prominent tabs would change depending on additional context from the user (e.g. If he/she frequently searches for Night Life or Arts & Entertainment venues, those would replace the Shopping/Services tab; or if a user is not logged in and it is night time, Night Life would replace the Shopping tab).

    The New Color Scheme.

    Yelp uses a unique coloring scheme for its ratings - red is used for the best as opposed to green. While this makes sense because of Yelp’s colors, it is inconsistent because red is also used in a negative connotation, like if a business was “Currently Closed”. This updated color scheme re-establishes that green is good, while making sure to avoid the use of Yelp’s red negatively.

    The Main Content.

    With the reduction of some clutter from categories, the main body has a bit more space. I added at-a-glance information for the top results: location, price, availability, and a modal map view. This allows users to quickly compare queries without further progressing into another page.

    The popular events section has been bumped to the top of the sidebar, as it is more effective when compared to the “Review of the Day” – individual reviews are much more specific, and is thus less useful to more users. New businesses are featured just after the main section, as opposed to individual reviews under “Recent Activity” for the same reason.
